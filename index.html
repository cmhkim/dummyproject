Paragraph: The End of The California Mission System

When misuderstandings happened, the Native Americans sometimes rebelled
against the padres. In 1775, around 800 Kumayaay attacked the mission at San Diego and burned
it to the ground. In 1785, the San Gabriel mission was attaced.

The struggle finally ended when the Mexicans gained their freedom from Spain in 1821.
One of the first things the Mexicans did was to break up the missions. The padres were allowed to keep the churches,
but the farmland and pueblos were given to the settlers.
Some of the land was supposed to be given to the Native Americans, but this didn't do them much good,
as their way of life was changed forever. Instead of living as hunters and gatherers, as they had done in the past,
they had now come to live as more of a European lifestyle. Also now that much of the land
belonged to other people, they were no longer free to hunt and gather wherever they pleased.
Many of the Native Americans who lived in the missions took jobs working on farms belonging to the new settlers.
California's economy, or way of life, now depends on agriculture, or farming, and would for many years to come.
